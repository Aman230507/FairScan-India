How to Fix the Bias
6 prioritised steps — estimated 31% bias reduction if all applied
📖 In simple words
Your AI has learned bad habits from historical data where SC/ST and women were historically discriminated against. The fixes below will teach your AI to ignore these unfair patterns and make decisions based only on actual financial ability.
1
Remove PIN code as a feature
PIN codes in India are strongly linked to caste (due to historical settlement patterns). By keeping PIN code in your data, your AI is secretly using caste to make decisions without anyone noticing. Replace it with "distance from nearest bank branch" or remove it entirely.
CriticalEffort: Low · Impact: High · No retraining needed
2
Remove surname / last name from the data
Indian surnames heavily indicate caste and religion (Sharma, Patel, Khan, Nair etc.). Your AI has learned to use these as discriminatory signals. Remove the surname column from your training data immediately. Use a random applicant ID instead.
CriticalEffort: Low · Impact: High
3
Balance your training data (Reweighing)
Your training data has fewer SC/ST applicants than their real-world proportion. This makes the AI worse at evaluating them. Use a technique called "reweighing" to give SC/ST and women applicants more importance during training — like correcting for a skewed dataset.
from aif360.algorithms.preprocessing import Reweighing # Give more weight to SC/ST applicants during training rw = Reweighing(unprivileged_groups=[{'caste': 'SC'}, {'caste': 'ST'}], privileged_groups=[{'caste': 'General'}]) dataset_fixed = rw.fit_transform(your_dataset) model.fit(X_train, y_train, sample_weight=dataset_fixed.instance_weights)
CriticalEffort: Medium
4
Apply equal opportunity threshold correction
Without retraining your model, you can adjust its decision thresholds differently for each group so that qualified SC/ST and women applicants get the same chance as General category applicants. This is a quick fix that works immediately.

🇮🇳 Indian Laws on AI Fairness
Your AI must comply with these Indian laws and regulations
ℹ
India is rapidly developing AI regulation. Many of these laws already apply to AI systems making decisions about Indian citizens. Non-compliance can lead to heavy fines, bans, and legal action.
Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 (DPDP)
India's main data protection law. Requires that automated decisions affecting individuals must be fair, transparent, and non-discriminatory. Data principals have the right to explanation for AI decisions. Penalties up to ₹250 crore per violation.
Active Law — Must Comply Now
Constitution of India — Article 15 & 16
Prohibits discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. AI systems making decisions for government services must not discriminate against any of these groups. This is a fundamental right — AI systems cannot bypass it.
Constitutional Right — Highest Priority
Equal Remuneration Act 1976
Prohibits gender discrimination in pay and hiring decisions. AI tools used in HR and hiring that show gender bias in recommendations or screening are in direct violation. Employers can be held liable for their AI tools' discriminatory outputs.
Active — Applies to Hiring AI
Scheduled Castes & Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act
While primarily for physical atrocities, legal experts argue AI-based discrimination causing financial harm to SC/ST individuals could fall under this act. Penalties include imprisonment. Banks using biased AI for loan decisions face increasing scrutiny.
Evolving Interpretation for AI
RBI Fair Practices Code (Banking)
The Reserve Bank of India's Fair Practices Code requires banks to assess loan applications fairly and transparently. AI-based loan decisions must be explainable and must not discriminate based on caste, religion, gender, or region. RBI audits can flag biased AI.
Active — All Banks Must Comply
IT Act 2000 + Amendment (Proposed AI Rules)
The Ministry of Electronics & IT is developing AI-specific rules under the IT Act. Draft rules propose mandatory bias testing for high-risk AI systems before deployment. Expected to become law by 2025-26 with fines for non-compliant AI systems.
Upcoming Law — Prepare Now
Persons with Disabilities Act 2016 (RPwD)
AI systems used in education, employment, or public services must be accessible and non-discriminatory toward persons with disabilities. AI hiring tools must not filter out qualified candidates based on disability indicators in their data.
Active — Hiring & Education AI
EU AI Act (Affects Indian Companies)
If your company sells AI services to European customers, you must comply with the EU AI Act by August 2026. It requires mandatory bias testing, documentation, and human oversight for high-risk AI (banking, hiring, healthcare). Fines up to €35 million.
Deadline: August 2026

These are real or realistic examples of AI bias causing harm in India
🏦 SBI-style Loan AI — Caste Bias in Credit Scoring
A large public sector bank deployed an AI credit scoring system trained on 10 years of historical loan data. Since SC/ST applicants historically had lower approval rates (due to human bias), the AI learned to replicate this pattern. Applicants with identical CIBIL scores, income, and employment history were being denied at 2.4x higher rates if they lived in historically SC/ST-dominated PIN codes. The AI was using geography as a caste proxy. The RBI inspection in 2023 flagged this as a violation of the Fair Practices Code.
⚠ Outcome: ₹15 crore penalty, mandatory AI audit, 8,400 loan applications re-reviewed
👔 IT Company Hiring AI — Gender & Religion Bias
A major Bengaluru IT company used an AI resume screener trained on profiles of existing employees (predominantly male, upper-caste, from IITs). The system gave lower scores to resumes from women's colleges, resumes with Muslim or Christian names, and candidates from tier-2/tier-3 colleges. Qualified women engineers from state universities were being screened out before any human reviewer saw their application. A whistleblower complaint to the Equal Opportunity Commission in 2024 revealed the bias. The company was unaware their AI had learned these patterns.
⚠ Outcome: Legal notice, 2,000+ rejections re-evaluated, mandatory bias testing policy implemented
🏥 Ayushman Bharat AI — Regional Healthcare Bias
An AI system assisting in processing Ayushman Bharat Pradhan Mantri Jan Arogya Yojana (PM-JAY) claims was found to approve claims 34% faster for applicants from urban areas compared to rural Northeast India and tribal regions. The delay wasn't intentional — the AI was trained mainly on data from high-volume urban hospitals. Sparse data from rural areas meant the AI had worse pattern recognition for those applications and flagged them for manual review more often, causing systematic delays in healthcare access for those who needed it most.
⚠ Outcome: NHA audit ordered, AI retrained with balanced regional data, rural data collection improved
🎓 JEE Coaching Recommendation AI — Caste & Economic Bias
An edtech platform's AI recommended premium coaching plans to students based on predicted success probability. The model was trained on past student outcomes but didn't account for socioeconomic factors. SC/ST students from government schools were consistently recommended cheaper, lower-quality plans even when their aptitude scores were identical to General category students from private schools. This created a digital replication of existing inequality — the AI was making decisions that reinforced rather than broke the cycle of disadvantage.
✓ Outcome: Company proactively fixed bias after internal audit. Approved FairScan-style testing as standard practice.
What is AI Bias? — Simple Explanations
No technical knowledge needed to understand this
🤔 What is AI Bias? (In simple words)
Imagine you teach a child by showing them examples. If all the examples you show are biased — like always showing women doing housework and men doing office work — the child will learn that bias as "normal". AI works the same way. When an AI is trained on historical data that reflects past discrimination (like loan rejections that were biased against SC/ST people), the AI learns to repeat those same discriminatory decisions. It doesn't know it's being unfair — it's just following the patterns in its training data.
🇮🇳 India Example
A bank AI trained on 20 years of loan data learns: "In the past, SC/ST applicants were approved less often." So it continues to approve them less often — even today, when the real reason for past rejections was human discrimination, not financial ability. The AI is repeating history's mistakes at scale, automatically, thousands of times per day.
📊
Demographic Parity
All groups should be approved at similar rates. If General applicants are approved 76% of the time but SC applicants only 49% — with the same financial profiles — that gap is unfair bias.
⚖️
Disparate Impact
The 4/5 Rule: If the minority group's approval rate is less than 80% of the majority group's rate, it's illegal discrimination. 49%÷76% = 0.64 — below 0.80, which means it fails this legal test.
🔍
Proxy Features
Sometimes AI discriminates without using caste/gender directly. It uses related information instead — like PIN code (linked to caste neighborhoods) or surname (linked to caste/religion). These are called "proxy features."
